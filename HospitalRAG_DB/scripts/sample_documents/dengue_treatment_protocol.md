# Dengue Fever Treatment Protocol

## Overview

Dengue is a mosquito-borne viral infection caused by the dengue virus
(DENV-1 to DENV-4). Most cases are self-limiting, but a subset progress
to severe dengue with plasma leakage, bleeding, and organ impairment.
Early recognition of warning signs is the single biggest factor in
preventing mortality.

## Diagnosis

Suspect dengue in any patient with acute fever (2-7 days) plus two or
more of: nausea/vomiting, rash, aches and pains, positive tourniquet
test, leukopenia, or any warning sign. Confirm with NS1 antigen testing
(days 1-5) or IgM/IgG serology (from day 5 onward).

## Warning Signs (Require Hospital Admission)

- Abdominal pain or tenderness
- Persistent vomiting
- Clinical fluid accumulation (ascites, pleural effusion)
- Mucosal bleeding
- Lethargy or restlessness
- Liver enlargement greater than 2 cm
- Rising hematocrit with rapid platelet drop

## Treatment Protocol

**Group A (no warning signs, able to tolerate oral fluids):**
Outpatient management. Oral rehydration, paracetamol for fever (max
4 g/day in adults). **Never use aspirin, ibuprofen, or other NSAIDs** --
they increase bleeding risk. Daily follow-up until afebrile for 48
hours, with platelet and hematocrit monitoring.

**Group B (warning signs present, or comorbidities):**
Hospital admission required. Start isotonic IV fluids (crystalloid) at
5-7 mL/kg/hour, titrated against hematocrit, urine output, and vital
signs. Monitor hematocrit every 4-6 hours during the critical phase
(typically days 3-7 of illness, around defervescence).

**Group C (severe dengue -- shock, severe bleeding, organ impairment):**
Immediate ICU-level care. Aggressive fluid resuscitation per the WHO
severe dengue protocol, blood product support for significant bleeding,
and management of the specific organ complication (hepatic, cardiac,
neurological, or renal).

## Discharge Criteria

- Afebrile for at least 24 hours without antipyretics
- Improving clinical status and appetite
- Stable or rising platelet count
- No respiratory distress
- Adequate urine output

## Key Contraindications

Do not give NSAIDs or intramuscular injections during the febrile or
critical phase -- both increase bleeding risk in a patient whose
platelet count may already be falling.
