# Hypertension Management Guideline (Adult Outpatients)

## Classification

- Normal: systolic < 120 and diastolic < 80 mmHg
- Elevated: systolic 120-129 and diastolic < 80 mmHg
- Stage 1: systolic 130-139 or diastolic 80-89 mmHg
- Stage 2: systolic >= 140 or diastolic >= 90 mmHg
- Hypertensive crisis: systolic > 180 and/or diastolic > 120 mmHg --
  requires immediate evaluation for end-organ damage.

Diagnosis requires an average of two or more properly measured seated
readings on two or more office visits, or confirmation with home/
ambulatory blood pressure monitoring.

## Lifestyle Management (all patients)

- Weight loss if overweight (aim for BMI < 25)
- DASH-style diet: reduced sodium (< 2.3 g/day, ideally < 1.5 g/day),
  increased potassium
- At least 150 minutes/week of moderate aerobic activity
- Limit alcohol: <= 2 drinks/day (men), <= 1 drink/day (women)
- Smoking cessation

## Pharmacological Treatment

Start medication for Stage 2 hypertension immediately, or for Stage 1
if 10-year cardiovascular risk is elevated or lifestyle changes alone
haven't reached target after 3-6 months.

First-line agents (any of the following, chosen by comorbidity):

- **ACE inhibitors** (e.g. lisinopril) or **ARBs** (e.g. losartan) --
  preferred in diabetes or chronic kidney disease; do not combine the
  two classes together.
- **Calcium channel blockers** (e.g. amlodipine) -- effective across
  most populations, including older adults.
- **Thiazide diuretics** (e.g. hydrochlorothiazide) -- inexpensive,
  well studied, useful first-line or add-on.

Most patients need two agents to reach target. Target is generally
< 130/80 mmHg for most adults, individualized for frailty and
comorbidity.

## Follow-Up

Recheck 2-4 weeks after starting or adjusting medication. Once
controlled, follow up every 3-6 months, checking renal function and
electrolytes for patients on ACE inhibitors/ARBs or diuretics.

## Hypertensive Emergency

Systolic > 180 and/or diastolic > 120 mmHg **with** signs of acute
end-organ damage (chest pain, altered mental status, visual changes,
worsening renal function) is a hypertensive emergency: admit for IV
antihypertensive therapy with gradual, controlled blood pressure
reduction. Without end-organ damage, this is a hypertensive urgency and
can typically be managed with oral medication adjustment and close
outpatient follow-up.
