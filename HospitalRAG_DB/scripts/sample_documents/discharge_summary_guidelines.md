# Discharge Summary Documentation Guidelines

## Purpose

A discharge summary is the primary handoff document between inpatient
care and the next care setting (home, primary care, rehabilitation, or
a skilled nursing facility). Incomplete discharge summaries are a
leading cause of post-discharge adverse events and readmissions.

## Required Sections

1. **Admission details** -- admission date, discharge date, admitting
   diagnosis, discharging diagnosis (if different).
2. **Hospital course** -- a concise narrative of what happened during
   admission: key findings, procedures, complications, and their
   resolution. Avoid a day-by-day log; summarize.
3. **Discharge medications** -- a complete reconciled list, clearly
   marking new medications, changed doses, and discontinued
   medications, with the reason for each change.
4. **Pending results** -- any labs, cultures, or imaging still pending
   at discharge, with instructions for who is responsible for
   following up.
5. **Follow-up appointments** -- specific provider, timeframe, and
   reason for each follow-up.
6. **Patient instructions** -- activity restrictions, diet, wound care,
   warning signs that should prompt return to the Emergency Department.
7. **Functional/cognitive status at discharge**, especially for older
   adults or anyone discharged to a lower level of care than their
   baseline.

## Timing

The discharge summary should be completed within 24 hours of discharge
whenever possible, and **must** be completed before any scheduled
follow-up appointment that falls within that window. A copy must be
sent to the primary care provider of record.

## Medication Reconciliation

Medication reconciliation at discharge must compare three lists: home
medications on admission, inpatient medications, and the discharge
medication list. Every discrepancy must be explicitly resolved and
documented, not silently dropped.

## Common Deficiencies to Avoid

- Vague follow-up instructions ("follow up with your doctor" without a
  named provider or timeframe)
- Missing rationale for medication changes
- Pending test results not flagged for follow-up
- Discharge summary not reaching the outpatient provider before the
  patient's first follow-up visit
